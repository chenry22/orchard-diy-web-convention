Introduction

In an age of AI, optimization, attention, social media, and speed over everything, it can be easy to forget what the web used to be. Though we have never really acheived the idyllic claims of total information availability and complete global connection, now it doesn't even feel like those are really our goals anymore. Somewhere along the way, people forgot about the point of this new form of connection, or, really, they stopped being taught.

The days of simple, personal, handmade websites are by no means gone, and the web (and the internet in general) is still a powerful platform used for expression, creation, and the distribution of interesting and important ideas. But there's definitely... less of this- at the very least, you have to look for it to find it. Which leads us to ORCHARD, and this web convention, and this introduction.

There's something to be said about the value of understanding the way things really work, and deeply owning the things you create. That's our goal, I guess, at least with this event. Obviously we can't teach anyone all that much about web design/development in just a few hours, but that's not really the point. It's more about knowing that you can do this, and that you can do it without AI, and thinking about the how this experience is necessarily different. There's a level of intimacy you achieve with having to consider all these little (sometimes annoying) design decisions, arranging pixels in a way that's specifically satisfying to you, wanting to do something and not knowing how to and then eventually (hopefully) figuring it out.

And to be honest, hanging out and playing around with websites just sounds like a good time. To create alone is to know yourself more deeply, and to create with others is to try and become something more than yourself.
